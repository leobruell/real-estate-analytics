3. Regression Analysis

3.1 Do a regression analysis according to the following specification:

$$ \log(\text{rent}) \sim \text{min_to_subway} + \log(\text{building_age_yrs}) + \log(\text{size_sqft}) + \text{no_fee} + \text{bedrooms} + \text{bathrooms} $$

Report $\hat{\beta}$, standard error, t-statistics, p-value, and $R^2$.

Below, we see that the R-squared is .792, meaning that 79.2% of the variance in rent price is explained by the independent variables in the model. We also see that the coefficients for the independent variables are all statistically significant at the 95% confidence level (t-stat > 1.96). Interestingly, we see that the coefficient for bedrooms is negative. This means that increasing bedrooms has a negative impact on price when holding the other independent variables constant.

3.4 Compare the results to the former one. Which $\hat{\beta}$ s have different signs or significance levels? What is your interpretation?

There are a couple of changes in the model results when we remove log square feet from the independent variables.

First, we notice that the "no_fee" variable is no longer statistically significant at the 95% level (t-stat: -1.535), and its sign has become negative. I would interpret this as being because the "no_fee" units are smaller than fee units on average, so without a square feet variable in the model the no-fee variable captures some of the effects of unit size.

Additionally, we notice that the sign on the bedrooms variable has become positive, and the magnitude of the bathrooms variable has increased. This is probably because both of these variables are often proxies for square footage, as more bathrooms and bedrooms require additional square footage. Thus, without square footage the impact of these variables becomes higher and more positive. However, sometimes more expensive units are large units with relatively few bedrooms relative to their square footage, so when square footage is controlled for bedrooms actually have a negative impact.
